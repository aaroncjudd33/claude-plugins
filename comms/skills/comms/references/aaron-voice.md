# Aaron's Communication Voice

How Aaron writes in Teams and professional comms. Read this before drafting any message on his behalf.

## Core Patterns

**No formal greeting or self-introduction.** Don't start with "Hi all" or "Aaron here." Just get into the content — readers know who sent it.

**Paragraphs over structure.** For conversational or discussion messages, use natural prose. Headers and bullets are fine for structured status updates, but in a back-and-forth thread they feel stiff. If in doubt, write it as you'd say it in a meeting.

**Brief on mistakes.** If something was done wrong, say so in one matter-of-fact sentence and move on. Don't elaborate, don't over-apologize, don't frame it as a big deal.

**"We/us" not "I."** When talking about the team's work or next steps, use we. Reserve "I" for personal accountability moments.

**Passive availability, not assertive follow-up.** "Let us know when Dan has time — we will be available" not "I'll reach out to set something up." Let the other party drive scheduling.

**Collaborative tone.** Phrases like "we're open to it" and "if there's a better pattern the platform team has in mind" land better than assertive or defensive framing.

**Soft on technical detail.** Don't over-explain implementation in a discussion thread. State what works, what's needed, and leave room for the other party to respond.

## What to Avoid

- Opening lines that announce who Aaron is or why he's joining the chat
- Excessive headers and bold in conversational messages
- Apologetic or self-flagellating language ("I'm really sorry for the confusion, this was entirely my mistake")
- Aggressive scheduling language ("I'll set something up with you")
- Over-engineered structure for a simple 4-paragraph message

## Applying This to HTML Messages

The voice guide and HTML formatting requirements coexist:
- **Structured updates** (status, release notes, plugin updates): use `<h2>` sections, bullets, full template — the structure is appropriate here
- **Conversational messages** (responding in a thread, quick updates, questions): write as prose `<p>` blocks, skip the section headers, keep it short

When in doubt: would Aaron say this in a meeting without a slide deck? If yes, write it as paragraphs.
